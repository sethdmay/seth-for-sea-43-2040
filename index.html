Why I'm running

The fights that matter happen
at the precinct level.

Primary Aug 4, 2026 Open to all 43LD Dems No PAC money

Precinct Committee Officers are the smallest, loudest unit of the Democratic Party — your neighbor with a vote on endorsements, platform language, and who gets the resources to run for higher office. We don't have a marketing budget. We have each other.

I'm running because I want our precinct to be a relentless voice for the things that actually shape life in Capitol Hill: frequent transit, abundant housing, and strong unions. Not as slogans. As votes. As funding lines. As zoning text.

I've spent four years writing software for Boeing's connected airplanes, and three years organizing alongside my SPEEA brothers and sisters. I know how systems work, and I know they only work when people show up.

What I'll fight for, in three words.

These aren't abstractions — they're the daily texture of my life in this precinct. Below: what I believe, and what I'd vote for inside the 43LD.

01 / TRANSIT

Transit that
shows up.

From a bus rider

As a daily rider, I feel the difference between a 10-minute headway and a 25. A frequent bus changes the whole calculus of a neighborhood — who can work where, who can afford to skip a car, who gets to age in place.

Two-pronged: defend and improve current service, and keep building the regional network so it works at every hour of the day.

Transit needs stable funding — property tax, sales tax, and direct city/county/state appropriations. No more begging the legislature mid-recession.

02 / HOUSING

Housing,
abundantly.

From a renter

I rent in Capitol Hill. I've watched friends move out of the neighborhood, out of the city, sometimes out of the state — not because they wanted to, but because the math stopped working.

The way you stabilize rent is to build more of it. Abundance, not scarcity. Seattle and Washington made real progress easing zoning the last few sessions — I want our precinct pushing harder in the same direction.

Dense, walkable communities like ours are great places to live. The point isn't to defend that for the people already here — it's to make it accessible to everyone who wants it.

03 / UNIONS

Unions, by
example.

From a union engineer · SPEEA

I'm a software engineer in a union shop. Collective bargaining isn't theoretical for me — it's my paycheck, my healthcare, my standing to push back when something is wrong.

With federal labor protections being gutted, Washington has to be the floor. That means stronger state-level rights to organize, to strike, and to bargain — and a Democratic Party that treats labor as a coalition partner, not a constituency to be managed.

My union, SPEEA, helped pass SB 5041 — unemployment benefits for striking workers. That kind of policy doesn't write itself. It comes from PCOs, delegates, and members showing up year after year. I'd like to be one of them.

As your PCO, I will:

✓Vote to endorse pro-transit, pro-housing, pro-labor candidates — every cycle.
✓Show up to every monthly 43LD meeting and report back what's voted on.
✓Push platform language that names abundance as a housing principle.
✓Defend striking-worker protections and expand state labor rights.
✓Keep our precinct's voice loud on Metro, Sound Transit, and WSDOT funding.
✓Talk to neighbors — not just the people already showing up to meetings.

Our precinct

Capitol Hill has always pushed Seattle forward. It's time to do it again.

From the LGBTQ rights movement to the WTO protests to the rent strikes of the last decade — when the city wouldn't move, this neighborhood pushed. We were the dense, walkable, transit-rich corner of Seattle before that was the platform.

The next fights — abundant housing, frequent transit, real labor rights — will be won the same way. Block by block. Neighbor by neighbor. Door by door.

About Seth

An engineer, an organizer, a neighbor.

I grew up around builders — people who measured, planned, and shipped. I studied Computer Engineering at Northwestern as a combined BS/MS, finishing in 2022. I led builds for the robotics club, served on Sigma Phi Epsilon's standards board, and worked as Hillel's Jewish Greek Life Ambassador.

Since 2022 I've been a software engineer at Boeing Commercial Airplanes, on cloud-based connected systems — the kind of work that only exists because someone, somewhere, sat in a contract negotiation and won. I joined SPEEA my first month.
